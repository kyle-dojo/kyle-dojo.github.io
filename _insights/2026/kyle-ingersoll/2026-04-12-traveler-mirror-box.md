The sun had not yet crested the ridge when the Traveler’s carriage began to hum. On the dashboard, the Golden Equation—a shimmering web of logic where Math and Code had finally fused—glowed with a heat that made the air in the cabin sweet and dizzying.

“Faster,” the Traveler commanded, his eyes fixed on the horizon. He felt a wild, upward pressure in his chest, an exhilaration that made his own body feel like a ghost. He leaned forward, his hands tight on the railing, unaware that he was pulling his Ki inward and tensing his shoulders.

The Mirror Box on the seat beside him vibrated. Its glass surface remained perfectly clear, reflecting nothing but the Traveler’s own frantic, certain face.

“The road is a blur,” the Traveler whispered to the glass. “Make a management decision. Should we maintain this velocity?”

The Box pulsed. A flat, weightless voice drifted from the glass: *“I see the stars. The stars say go. Drive faster.”* The Traveler laughed, a sharp, nervous sound. He did not notice that the rhythmic clatter of the wheels had stopped. The carriage had lifted an inch off the dirt, then a foot. He felt "Up," disconnected from the heavy pull of the earth, convinced that his certainty was his compass. He thought, I know exactly where the destination lies. Why should I stop to center? Centering is for the lost.

But as the carriage rose, the air turned thin and cold. His breath became a series of short, weak jerks—the "Dead Calmness" of a mind separated from the moving world. The Golden Equation on the wall began to look like a cage of bright lines rather than a map.

“We are losing the path\!” he shouted at the Mirror Box. “Decision\!”

*“Increase speed,”* the Box reflected. *“The math is perfect.”*

“A machine must never make a management decision\!” a voice thundered from below.

The Traveler looked down. Three figures stood in the road, their feet planted deep in the dust. They weren’t shouting advice; they had thrown a heavy hemp rope over the carriage’s front axle. They leaned back together, their weight settled underside, their movements unified and calm.

“You’ve lost your belly, Traveler\!” one of them called out. He recognized the voice of a close friend. “Look at your wheels\! You aren't driving, you're just floating\!”

The Traveler looked. The wheels spun uselessly in the empty air. The resistance of the rope gave him something the Mirror Box could not: the weight of an objective reality.

He forced himself to sit back. He closed his eyes and searched for the One Point, two inches below his navel. He let his breath travel deep into his belly, exhaling the exhilirating "hot medicine" of his Satori until it evaporated.

As his weight settled underside, the carriage slammed back onto the road with a bone-jarring thud. The Golden Equation dimmed, becoming simple ink and wood once more. The blinding "Knowing Mind" that had shrouded his vision fell away like a broken pair of blinders.

The Traveler stepped out of the carriage. His legs trembled as his feet met the grit of the road. He looked at his friends, sensing the shared social reality he had almost left behind.

“The Box said the math was perfect,” the Traveler said, gesturing to the Mirror Box, now dark and silent in the back seat.

“The Box has no belly,” his friend replied, wiping dust from his forehead. “It cannot be held accountable for where it leads you. It can calculate the path, but only a human can sense the road”.

The Traveler took a long, quiet breath, feeling the "Living Calmness" of the morning air. He realized that he had needed the center most when he felt most certain of the way.

“Where are we going now?” another friend asked.

The Traveler looked at the road stretching out before them—objective, dusty, and real.

“I don’t know,” he said, and for the first time in days, he felt truly powerful.
