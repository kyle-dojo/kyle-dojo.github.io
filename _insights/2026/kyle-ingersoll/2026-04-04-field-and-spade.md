A traveler came upon a monk sitting at the edge of a vast, stump-ridden field. The traveler wore a heavy cloak of purple silk, embroidered with the names of all the things he was supposed to become. He was sweating under the weight.

"I am going to the City of Light," the traveler said, his voice tight. "But I am stuck in this mud. I have spent years preparing to be the Great Architect of the North, yet here I am, wasting my time in a fallow field."

The monk did not look up. He was prying at a thick, stubborn root with an iron spade.

"The mud is just mud," the monk said. "The spade is just iron."

The traveler gripped his purple cloak. "But look at this silk! I pushed myself to the brink to earn it. If I do not reach the City, this silk is a lie. My life is a waste of potential."

The monk struck the root. *Thud.* "The silk is just thread," the monk said. "The City is just stone. Your 'potential' is just a ghost standing behind you. He cannot help you dig."

The traveler grew silent. The wind blew cold across the empty field. Without the "Specialness" of his journey, he felt a sudden, hollow loneliness. He looked at the vast forest ahead and felt the crushing weight of the distance.

"If the City is just stone," the traveler asked, "why do you bother to dig?"

The monk stopped and handed the traveler a spare spade.

"Because the next root is right here," the monk said. "And the ground doesn't care about your cloak."

The traveler stood still for a long time. Then, he unbuckled the purple silk and let it fall into the mud. He felt the cold on his skin, and for the first time in years, he felt the true lightness of his own bones.

He didn't look at the horizon. He didn't think about the City. He simply stepped into the mud, gripped the iron, and felt the sharp, clean truth of the spade meeting the wood.

The ghost was gone. There was only the strike.
